Ryan Sarver is a VC — angel investor, Redpoint Ventures, ex-Twitter platform lead. He's in the middle of a fundraise, sitting on boards, helping portfolio companies. He tried building an AI chief of staff when the first AI APIs came out but couldn't make it work. When OpenClaw launched, he went deep. His system "Stella" is now more capable than any human chief of staff he's worked with — never forgets commitments, handles small stuff without being asked, flags important stuff without being told, and gets better every week.

Section 01
What Makes a Great Chief of Staff?

Before building anything, Sarver defined what the best human chiefs of staff actually did for him:

  • Filtered noise so only the right things reached him
  • Made sure he walked into every meeting prepared and nothing fell through after
  • Kept the full picture of what was in flight and flagged slipping items
  • Tracked relationships and knew where things stood with every important person
  • Created daily and weekly rhythm that kept everything moving
The job isn't about doing the work. It's about making sure the right work happens, nothing falls through, and the principal always has context.
Section 02
Memory — The Foundation

"Session memory is a lie." Stella uses two layers of persistent memory:

1. Daily notes — one markdown file per day (memory/YYYY-MM-DD.md). A raw log of everything: meetings, decisions, tasks, context. Auto-pulled from sessions at end of day.

2. Long-term memory (MEMORY.md) — curated by Stella herself. Key people, active projects, lessons learned. Synthesized from daily notes and read on startup.

All lives in flat markdown files — not a database. You can open, read, edit, and back up to git. No abstraction layer.

Example: Managing a fundraise with 100+ LP contacts across countries. Stella tracks the full pipeline, keeps context on each LP, knows where every relationship stands. For first meetings — researches the fund and recent content, preps with findings and tailored talking points. For ongoing relationships — knows pipeline position, last discussion, key issues raised.
Section 03
Kaizen — The System Improves Itself

Every Friday a cron job runs research: scans the OpenClaw community, checks new patterns, saves findings to kaizen-research files. Sunday morning: review together, surfaces the top ideas worth trying.

Stella also learns from daily interactions. If Sarver keeps correcting something, she captures the pattern and suggests a fix to her own instructions. A human chief of staff can't scan hundreds of builders and cross-reference what they're doing every week.

Drives continuous refactoring. First versions are always too complicated. A smaller trusted system beats a bigger one you route around.
Section 04
Meeting Prep & Follow-Through

60 minutes before every external meeting, a brief arrives via WhatsApp. It pulls prior meeting notes, checks email threads, finds open action items. For LP meetings: pipeline stage, deck version they've seen, their questions, his commitments.

Post-meeting: processes transcripts via the Granola API. Extracts action items and pushes them to Todoist. Commitments by others are tracked in per-person markdown files. If someone owes something for 3 weeks with no update — the system knows.

Everything feeds back into memory for next meeting.

Section 05
Task & Priority Management

A comprehensive picture lives in structured markdown (the source of truth). Important near-term items get synced to Todoist. Chat interface + focused app = better than either alone.

Evening task sweep: what's due, overdue, sitting too long, coming this week. Flags patterns ("this has been rolled forward 5 days"). High-stakes meeting with no prep done? Says so. Nothing needs attention? Silence.

The system earns trust by knowing when to stay quiet. If there's nothing to flag — silence. That's a feature, not a bug.
Section 06
Stakeholder & Relationship Context

Persistent structured context on every person, company, and project: relationship history, last touchpoint, open commitments, what they care about.

Memory applied to relationships is where this compounds most visibly over time. Every conversation builds on the last. Nothing gets dropped.
Section 07
Information Filtering

Email and calendar triage across personal and work Gmail. Auto-pulls expense receipts into quarterly tracking. Extracts travel itineraries from booking confirmations. Drafts follow-up emails in his voice.

The system turns information chaos into structured, actionable data — without being asked.

Section 08
Operational Rhythm

Morning brief at 9am, evening wrap at 6pm — both via WhatsApp.

  • Morning: top priorities, overdue tasks, calendar, attention items
  • Evening: what happened, what stalled, prep for tomorrow

If nothing needs to be said — silence. The rhythm is reliable but not noisy.

Section 09
Research & Intelligence

Weekly curated digest of X accounts and newsletters. Organized into tiers: AI researchers, VCs, founders, operators. Scored by engagement, filtered for relevance.

Turns 45 minutes of scrolling into a few minutes of reading. The signal-to-noise ratio is everything.
Section 10
Judgement vs. Predictability

The core design rule: LLMs handle judgment, scripts handle everything else.

  • Deterministic work (reading files, calling APIs, sending messages, comparing timestamps) → Python
  • LLM work → synthesis, prioritization, drafting, reasoning
Push deterministic work through an LLM and it breaks unpredictably. The line between "needs judgment" and "just needs execution" is the most important architectural decision.
Section 11
Where This Is Going
"I didn't get the best assistant I've ever had by asking better questions. I got it by giving the system a better operating model."

The insight isn't about prompting. It's about building the right structure — memory, rhythm, tools, judgment boundaries — and letting the system operate within it. The operating model is the product.

What Leon Can Learn

Actionable ideas from Stella to make Hermes even stronger
Takeaway 01

Adopt the Kaizen Loop

Weekly automated research scan of Claude/Anthropic community + builder patterns. Friday scrape → Sunday review. Let Hermes find its own improvements.

Takeaway 02

Self-Correction Capture

When you repeatedly correct Hermes on something, it should detect the pattern and propose a skill/instruction update — not just comply in the moment.

Takeaway 03

Per-Person Commitment Tracking

Structured markdown file per key contact (KKW leaders, partners). Track: last touchpoint, open commitments from them, open commitments to them, relationship notes.

Takeaway 04

Meeting Prep Automation

60-min pre-meeting briefs via WhatsApp. Pull prior notes, open items, relationship context. Especially valuable for recurring 1:1s with KKW leaders.

Takeaway 05

Sharpen the LLM vs. Script Boundary

Audit Hermes for places where deterministic work is being pushed through Claude. Move timestamp comparisons, file operations, API calls to pure Python. Reserve LLM for synthesis and judgment.

Takeaway 06

Evening Task Sweep

Daily automated review: what's overdue, what's been sitting too long, what's coming tomorrow. Flag patterns. If nothing needs attention — silence.
